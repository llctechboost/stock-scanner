# Complete Guide to Chart Patterns & Trading Terms
## Everything You Need to Know for Technical Analysis & Swing Trading

---

## PART 1: FOUNDATIONAL CONCEPTS

### What is Technical Analysis?
Technical analysis is the study of price and volume data on charts to identify patterns, trends, and potential trading opportunities. Unlike fundamental analysis (which looks at earnings, revenue, etc.), technical analysis focuses purely on what the chart is telling you about supply and demand.

### Price Action Basics
Every stock chart shows the battle between buyers (demand) and sellers (supply). When buyers are more aggressive, prices go up. When sellers are more aggressive, prices go down. The chart is simply a visual record of this battle over time.

### Candlestick Charts
The most common chart type. Each candlestick represents one time period (1 day on a daily chart).
- **Green/White candle**: Price closed HIGHER than it opened (bullish)
- **Red/Black candle**: Price closed LOWER than it opened (bearish)
- **Body**: The thick part between open and close
- **Wicks/Shadows**: The thin lines above and below the body showing the high and low
- **Doji**: When open and close are nearly equal — shows indecision
- **Hammer**: Small body at top with long lower wick — potential reversal signal after a decline
- **Shooting Star**: Small body at bottom with long upper wick — potential reversal after an advance
- **Engulfing Pattern**: A large candle that completely engulfs the previous candle's body

### Volume
Volume is the number of shares traded in a given period. It's the single most important confirming indicator.
- **Volume confirms price**: A breakout on heavy volume is more reliable than one on light volume
- **Average volume**: The typical number of shares traded per day (usually 50-day average)
- **Volume dry-up**: When volume decreases significantly, often seen before breakouts
- **Volume spike**: A sudden surge in trading activity, often 2-3x or more above average
- **Up volume vs down volume**: Volume on up days vs down days tells you who's in control

### Trend
- **Uptrend**: Series of higher highs and higher lows — the stock is going up
- **Downtrend**: Series of lower highs and lower lows — the stock is going down
- **Sideways/Range**: Price bouncing between support and resistance — consolidation
- **Trend is your friend**: The #1 rule — trade in the direction of the prevailing trend

### Support and Resistance
- **Support**: A price level where buying demand is strong enough to prevent further decline. Think of it as a floor.
- **Resistance**: A price level where selling pressure is strong enough to prevent further advance. Think of it as a ceiling.
- **When support breaks, it becomes resistance** (and vice versa)
- **The more times a level is tested, the more significant it becomes**

### Moving Averages
Moving averages smooth out price data to show the trend direction.
- **50-day Moving Average (50 MA)**: The average closing price over the last 50 days. Institutional investors watch this closely.
- **200-day Moving Average (200 MA)**: The long-term trend indicator. Above = bullish, below = bearish.
- **10-day and 21-day MA**: Short-term trend indicators used by swing traders
- **Golden Cross**: When the 50 MA crosses above the 200 MA — bullish long-term signal
- **Death Cross**: When the 50 MA crosses below the 200 MA — bearish long-term signal
- **EMA (Exponential Moving Average)**: Gives more weight to recent prices, reacts faster

## PART 2: BASE PATTERNS (The Building Blocks)

A "base" is a consolidation period where a stock digests its prior advance. Bases are where institutional investors accumulate shares quietly before the next leg up. Learning to identify bases is the #1 skill in growth stock trading.

### Cup and Handle (William O'Neil's Signature Pattern)
The most reliable base pattern, identified by William O'Neil, founder of Investor's Business Daily.
- **Shape**: Looks like a coffee cup with a handle on the right side
- **Cup depth**: Typically 12-35% from peak to trough (deeper in bear markets)
- **Cup length**: Usually 7 weeks to 65 weeks
- **Handle**: A slight drift downward on the right side of the cup, usually 1-2 weeks
- **Handle should drift down**: Not up — downward drift shakes out weak holders
- **Handle volume**: Should dry up — light volume shows selling pressure is exhausted
- **Buy point (pivot)**: The highest point in the handle + $0.10
- **Volume on breakout**: Should be at least 40-50% above average — institutional buying confirmation
- **Why it works**: The cup represents a correction where weak holders sell. The handle is a final shakeout. When the stock breaks above the handle, all the selling is done and institutions drive it higher.

### Flat Base
- **Shape**: A sideways consolidation that looks like a flat rectangle
- **Depth**: Usually only 10-15% — very tight
- **Length**: Minimum 5 weeks
- **Significance**: Shows incredible strength — the stock barely pulled back despite market pressure
- **Buy point**: The high of the flat base + $0.10
- **Often forms after a breakout**: A stock breaks out, advances 20-25%, then forms a flat base before the next leg

### Double Bottom
- **Shape**: Looks like a "W" — two distinct low points
- **Second low**: Should undercut the first low slightly (shakes out stop-loss orders)
- **Middle peak**: The high point between the two lows
- **Buy point**: The middle peak + $0.10
- **Volume**: Should dry up at the second low and surge on the breakout
- **Why the undercut matters**: When the second low dips below the first, it triggers stop-losses and scares out weak holders — this is the final shakeout before the advance

### Ascending Base
- **Shape**: Three pullbacks, each with a higher low than the previous
- **Each pullback**: Usually 10-20% deep
- **Length**: 9-16 weeks typically
- **Significance**: Shows strong buying on each dip — institutions are accumulating
- **Buy point**: The high of the pattern + $0.10

### High Tight Flag
- **The most powerful pattern** but also the rarest
- **Prerequisites**: Stock must have advanced 100-120% or more in 4-8 weeks
- **Flag**: Then corrects only 10-25% over 3-5 weeks
- **Significance**: A stock that doubles and barely pulls back has massive institutional demand
- **Buy point**: The high of the flag + $0.10
- **Success rate**: When this pattern works, it often leads to another 100%+ move

### IPO Base
- **Applies to newly public companies** (within first 2-3 years)
- **Rules are looser**: Can be shorter than traditional bases (as little as 2-3 weeks)
- **Deeper corrections allowed**: IPOs can correct 40-50% and still form valid bases
- **Why different**: New stocks don't have the trading history, so patterns form differently

### Saucer Base
- **Shape**: A very gradual, rounded bottom — like a shallow saucer
- **Length**: Usually 7+ weeks
- **Character**: Slow and steady recovery, not dramatic
- **Volume**: Gradually increases as the right side forms
- **Significance**: Institutions are patiently accumulating over an extended period

## PART 3: THE VCP (VOLATILITY CONTRACTION PATTERN)

### What is a VCP?
The Volatility Contraction Pattern was popularized by Mark Minervini, a US Investing Champion. It's one of the most powerful and reliable patterns for identifying stocks about to make big moves.

### The Core Concept
A VCP shows that selling pressure is drying up through a series of price contractions, each one tighter than the last. Think of it like a spring being compressed — each bounce is smaller until the selling is completely exhausted, and then the stock explodes upward.

### Anatomy of a VCP
- **T1 (First Contraction)**: The largest correction. Maybe 25% from the high.
- **T2 (Second Contraction)**: Smaller than T1. Maybe 15% — that's 60% as deep as T1.
- **T3 (Third Contraction)**: Even smaller. Maybe 8% — the tightest yet.
- **Pivot Point**: The top of the final (tightest) contraction — this is your buy point.

### What Makes a Good VCP
1. **Successive tighter contractions**: Each wave of selling should have a smaller price range than the previous. If T1 is 25%, T2 should be less (say 15%), and T3 even less (say 8%).
2. **Volume dry-up**: Volume should decrease significantly in the later contractions. Ideally 40-50%+ below the 50-day average. This shows sellers are exhausted.
3. **At least 2-3 contractions**: More contractions = more reliable signal.
4. **Price near the pivot**: The stock should be consolidating near its highs, not far below.
5. **Base depth**: Total correction from the high is typically 10-35%.
6. **Duration**: Usually forms over 3-12 weeks.

### Tightness Ratio
The tightness ratio measures how much each contraction narrows compared to the previous one. 
- T2/T1 = 60% means T2 was 60% as deep as T1 (good — it's tightening)
- T3/T2 = 40% means T3 was only 40% as deep as T2 (excellent — very tight)
- Ratios below 75% are good. Below 50% is excellent.

### Volume Dry-Up
- Measured as current volume vs the 50-day average volume
- 0.5x = volume is half the average (good dry-up)
- 0.3x = volume is 30% of average (excellent dry-up)
- 1.0x+ = volume is at or above average (not ideal — still active selling)

### The Breakout
- **Buy point**: Top of the last contraction (the pivot) + a small buffer
- **Volume confirmation**: Breakout day should have volume at least 40-50% above average
- **Gap ups are fine**: A gap up on volume from a VCP is a powerful signal
- **Don't chase**: If you miss the buy point by more than 5%, wait for the next setup

### Textbook VCP Characteristics
A "textbook" VCP has:
- Score of 7+ out of 10
- 3 or more contractions
- Each contraction tighter than the previous
- Within 15% of the 52-week high
- Clear volume dry-up

### Common VCP Mistakes
1. **Buying too early**: Don't buy during the contractions — wait for the pivot breakout
2. **Ignoring volume**: A breakout without volume is suspect
3. **Wide and loose**: If contractions aren't getting tighter, it's not a VCP
4. **Too deep**: If the total base depth exceeds 40%, it's probably not a VCP
5. **Fighting the trend**: VCPs work best in uptrending markets

## PART 4: CANSLIM METHODOLOGY

### What is CANSLIM?
CANSLIM is a growth stock selection method created by William O'Neil. Each letter stands for a key criterion:

### C — Current Quarterly Earnings
- Look for stocks with quarterly earnings growth of 25% or more year-over-year
- The higher the better — 50%, 100%, even 200% growth
- Accelerating earnings growth is the strongest signal (25% → 50% → 100%)

### A — Annual Earnings Growth
- Annual earnings should be growing 25%+ per year for the last 3-5 years
- Return on equity (ROE) should be 17% or higher
- Annual earnings should show a consistent upward trend

### N — New Products, New Management, New Highs
- Look for companies with something NEW driving growth
- New product or service, new management team, new industry trend
- The stock should be making or near new 52-week highs (buy strength, not weakness)

### S — Supply and Demand
- Smaller float = more explosive moves (fewer shares available)
- Look for volume surges on up days (institutional buying)
- Share buybacks are a positive — company reducing its own supply

### L — Leader or Laggard
- Buy the #1 or #2 stock in the strongest industry groups
- Use Relative Strength (RS) Rating — should be 80+ (ideally 90+)
- RS Rating of 95 means the stock is outperforming 95% of all stocks

### I — Institutional Sponsorship
- Increasing number of institutional owners (mutual funds, hedge funds)
- Look for quality institutions, not just quantity
- Recent quarters should show rising institutional ownership

### M — Market Direction
- 3 out of 4 stocks follow the general market direction
- Don't fight the market — buy in confirmed uptrends, raise cash in downtrends
- Follow-through days confirm new uptrends
- Distribution days warn of potential market tops

## PART 5: MARK MINERVINI'S SEPA (Specific Entry Point Analysis)

### The Trend Template
Before looking at any pattern, the stock must pass Minervini's trend template:
1. Stock price is above both the 150-day and 200-day moving average
2. The 150-day MA is above the 200-day MA
3. The 200-day MA is trending up for at least 1 month (ideally 4-5 months)
4. The 50-day MA is above both the 150-day and 200-day MA
5. Current price is at least 30% above the 52-week low (preferably 100%+)
6. Current price is within 25% of the 52-week high
7. RS Rating is 70+ (preferably 80+)

### Stage Analysis
Stocks move through 4 stages:
- **Stage 1 (Basing/Accumulation)**: Stock is flat, moving sideways. Smart money starts buying quietly.
- **Stage 2 (Advancing/Markup)**: The uptrend begins. This is where you want to buy. Price is above rising moving averages.
- **Stage 3 (Topping/Distribution)**: The uptrend stalls. Moving averages start to flatten. Volatility increases. Smart money starts selling.
- **Stage 4 (Declining/Markdown)**: The downtrend. Price falls below moving averages. Never buy in Stage 4.

**Only buy in Stage 2!** This is Minervini's #1 rule.

### Risk Management (The Most Important Part)
- **Stop loss**: Always set a stop loss. Maximum 7-8% below your buy point.
- **Position sizing**: Risk no more than 1-2% of your total portfolio on any single trade.
- **The R-multiple**: If you risk $1 per share (your stop loss distance), you want to make at least $3 (3:1 reward-to-risk ratio).
- **Cut losses quickly**: The single most important rule. Small losses are recoverable. Big losses are devastating.
  - A 10% loss requires 11% to recover
  - A 25% loss requires 33% to recover
  - A 50% loss requires 100% to recover (double your money just to break even!)

## PART 6: BREAKOUT TRADING

### What is a Breakout?
A breakout occurs when a stock moves above a defined resistance level (the pivot point) on increased volume. This signals that demand has overwhelmed supply and the stock is likely to move higher.

### Pivot Point / Buy Point
The specific price at which you should buy:
- Usually the highest point in the handle (cup with handle) or the top of the final contraction (VCP)
- Add $0.10 as a buffer above the actual high
- The pivot is your "line in the sand" — the stock must clear this level convincingly

### Volume on Breakout
- **Minimum 40-50% above average volume** on the breakout day
- Higher is better — 100%, 200% above average shows strong institutional commitment
- A breakout on below-average volume is a red flag — "low volume breakout" often fails

### The Buy Zone
- The ideal buy range is from the pivot point to 5% above the pivot
- Beyond 5%, you're "chasing" — the risk-reward deteriorates
- If you miss the buy zone, don't chase. Wait for the next setup.

### Gap Ups
- A stock that gaps up above the pivot on massive volume is extremely bullish
- This shows overnight demand was so strong that the stock opened well above the pivot
- Gap ups from proper bases are some of the most powerful buy signals

### Failed Breakouts
- Not every breakout works. About 40% of breakouts fail even in good markets.
- **Set your stop loss** at the pivot point or 7-8% below your entry
- A failed breakout that falls back below the pivot on heavy volume is a sell signal
- Failed breakouts in heavy distribution markets are common — respect the market trend

### Shakeouts
- Sometimes a stock will dip just below the pivot or support level before rallying hard
- This "shakeout" is designed to trigger stop-losses and scare out weak holders
- After the shakeout, if the stock recovers quickly on volume, it can be a buying opportunity
- The double bottom pattern specifically uses this undercut-and-recover concept

## PART 7: KEY INDICATORS & METRICS

### Relative Strength (RS) Rating
- Measures a stock's price performance vs all other stocks over the last 12 months
- Scale of 1-99 (99 = top 1% of all stocks)
- **80+ is good, 90+ is great, 95+ is exceptional**
- Leaders have high RS ratings. Laggards have low RS ratings.

### RS Line
- A line on the chart that compares the stock's performance to the S&P 500
- **Rising RS line** = outperforming the market (bullish)
- **Falling RS line** = underperforming the market (bearish)
- New high in RS line before new high in price = very bullish (relative strength precedes absolute strength)

### Accumulation/Distribution Rating
- Measures whether institutional investors are buying (accumulating) or selling (distributing)
- Scale of A to E (A = heavy accumulation, E = heavy distribution)
- A or B rating is ideal for buy candidates

### Earnings Per Share (EPS) Rating
- Measures a company's earnings growth vs all other companies
- Scale of 1-99
- Look for 80+ (strong earnings growth)

### On-Balance Volume (OBV)
- Cumulative indicator that adds volume on up days and subtracts on down days
- Rising OBV = bullish (more buying pressure)
- Falling OBV = bearish (more selling pressure)
- OBV divergence (price flat but OBV rising) can signal an upcoming breakout

### VWAP (Volume Weighted Average Price)
- The average price weighted by volume throughout the day
- Institutional traders use VWAP as a benchmark
- Price above VWAP = bullish intraday bias
- Price below VWAP = bearish intraday bias

## PART 8: MARKET HEALTH INDICATORS

### Follow-Through Day (FTD)
- Confirms a new market uptrend after a correction
- Occurs on day 4 or later of an attempted rally
- Requires a major index to gain 1.25%+ on volume higher than the previous day
- Not all FTDs work, but all market rallies start with one

### Distribution Days
- A day where a major index drops 0.2%+ on higher volume than the previous day
- Distribution = institutional selling
- 4-5 distribution days in a 25-day window is a warning sign
- Clustering of distribution days is even more dangerous

### Market Breadth
- How many stocks are participating in the move
- **Advance-Decline Line**: If rising, more stocks advancing than declining (healthy)
- **New Highs vs New Lows**: More new highs = healthy market
- Narrow market (only a few stocks leading) is unsustainable

### Sector Rotation
- Money flows between sectors based on the economic cycle
- **Early cycle**: Technology, Consumer Discretionary
- **Mid cycle**: Industrials, Materials, Energy
- **Late cycle**: Healthcare, Utilities, Consumer Staples
- Follow the money — buy the leading stocks in the leading sectors

## PART 9: PRACTICAL TRADING RULES

### The 8 Rules of Mark Minervini
1. **Trend is everything** — Only buy Stage 2 uptrending stocks
2. **Buy at the right time** — Use proper buy points from bases and VCPs
3. **Cut losses quickly** — Maximum 7-8% loss from your buy point
4. **Let winners run** — Don't sell too early; trail your stops
5. **Risk management first** — Never risk more than 1-2% of your portfolio per trade
6. **The market is always right** — Don't argue with price action
7. **Be selective** — Quality over quantity; only the best setups
8. **Have a plan** — Know your entry, stop, and target before you trade

### When to Sell for Profit
- **20-25% rule**: Take some profits when a stock is 20-25% above your buy point
- **Climax top**: Rapid acceleration after a long advance, often on huge volume
- **Break of 50-day MA**: If a stock breaks below its 50-day MA on volume after a long run
- **Exhaustion gap**: A gap up after an extended advance, followed by a reversal
- **9-month rule**: Most big stock moves last 12-18 months from the initial breakout

### Position Sizing Formula
- Decide how much you're willing to lose (e.g., 1% of $100,000 = $1,000)
- Determine your stop loss distance (e.g., $5 per share below your entry)
- Position size = Risk amount / Stop distance = $1,000 / $5 = 200 shares
- This ensures no single trade can hurt you badly

## GLOSSARY OF KEY TERMS

- **Base**: A consolidation period where a stock trades sideways, building a foundation for the next advance
- **Breakout**: When price moves above resistance on increased volume
- **Buy Point / Pivot**: The specific price to buy, usually the top of a handle or pattern
- **Consolidation**: A pause in the trend where price moves sideways
- **Distribution**: Institutional selling — heavy volume on down days
- **Accumulation**: Institutional buying — heavy volume on up days
- **Float**: The number of shares available for public trading
- **Gap**: When a stock opens significantly above or below the previous close
- **Handle**: The final short-term pullback before a breakout (in cup-with-handle pattern)
- **Institutional Investor**: Mutual funds, hedge funds, pension funds — the big money
- **Pivot Point**: See Buy Point
- **Pocket Pivot**: A buy signal where up-day volume exceeds any down-day volume in the prior 10 days
- **Prior Resistance**: A level that was previously overhead resistance — now acts as support
- **Pullback**: A temporary decline within an uptrend
- **Relative Strength**: How a stock performs compared to the overall market
- **Shakeout**: A sudden dip below support designed to scare out weak holders before a rally
- **Tight Action**: When a stock trades in a very narrow range — shows supply/demand equilibrium
- **Volatility Contraction**: When price swings get progressively smaller — setup for a big move
- **Whipsaw**: When a stock triggers a buy or sell signal, then immediately reverses
